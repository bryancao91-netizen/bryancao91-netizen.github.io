Background & History

The Story Behind the I-710 Stub

Understanding why this freeway exists — and why it was never completed — is essential context for the proposed transformation.

Original Intent

The SR-710 freeway extension was originally designed to connect Interstate 10 in Monterey Park to Interstate 210 in Pasadena, creating a continuous north-south corridor through the San Gabriel Valley. The southern segment was completed in the 1960s, but the full 4.5-mile extension was never finished — leaving behind a disconnected stub that has sat incomplete for over half a century.

Why It Was Never Finished

The proposed northern extension would have destroyed nearly 1,000 homes, displaced approximately 24,000 residents, and cut through established historic neighborhoods. Decades of sustained community opposition, environmental review challenges, and legal battles culminated in a 1999 federal court injunction halting further progress. The California Transportation Commission officially cancelled the project in June 2022

Environmental Justice Context

The I-710 corridor is one of the most polluted transportation corridors in the United States, carrying over 200,000 vehicles per day as a primary freight route serving the Ports of Los Angeles and Long Beach. Diesel emissions along the corridor have caused severe air quality impacts disproportionately affecting communities of color, making environmental justice a central concern in every decision about the stub's future.

What Happens to the Land Now

Following the 2022 cancellation, funding previously allocated to freeway expansion was redirected toward local street improvements and multimodal investments. In Pasadena, Caltrans transferred control of the northern stub to the city with accompanying transportation funds. The City of Alhambra is now actively considering repurposing the southern stub into an arterial street with pedestrian and bicycle facilities — the foundation this project builds upon.
